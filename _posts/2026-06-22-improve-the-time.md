On a recent afternoon in Market Harborough, I stood in front of the old stone sundial on St. Dionysius church. Carved above the shadow line are three simple words: *Improve the Time.*

It stopped me cold.

In over a decade working in UK/US tech, I’ve rarely heard anyone talk about time this way. We obsess over optimizing it, accelerating it, or hacking it. Almost never improving it.

For me, that phrase hit harder because of where I come from.

I grew up in a culture that deeply values silence. The saying I heard again and again was: *Only the ripened wheat stalks bow their heads.* Real substance doesn’t need to announce itself.  

That mindset served me well as a Product Manager. For years I stayed low, focused on shipping products, scaling teams, and doing the work quietly. I believed strong execution would speak for itself.

But after more than ten years in the UK, I’ve come to see the limits of that approach.

In Western tech culture, visibility often *is* value. The loudest voices, the sharpest critiques, and the most aggressive self-promoters tend to get rewarded. Staying completely silent, I’ve realized, can slide from humility into avoidance. If you’ve spent years in the trenches, shipping real products and seeing what works and what fails, then withholding those lessons isn’t humble. It’s wasteful.

The internet is drowning in noise. Much of what passes for "thought leadership" is recycled jargon written for algorithms, not practitioners.

That’s why this blog exists.

This isn’t a personal brand exercise or a platform for hot takes. It’s my attempt to **improve the time**.

I want to write useful, grounded pieces about product craft, organizational reality, and strategic trade-offs. These are the kinds of insights that come from actually doing the work, not just talking about it. I plan to stay honest and low on bullshit.

I’m no longer staying quiet. It’s time to build in the open.
