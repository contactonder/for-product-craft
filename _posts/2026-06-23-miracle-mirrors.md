When the Tunisian football federation sacked head coach Sabri Lamouchi immediately after a 5-1 loss to Sweden, they expected a reset. Days later, under a new coach, they lost 4-0 to Japan.

Sweden suffered an identical 5-1 thrashing later in the tournament against the Netherlands. Their response? They kept their structure, stayed calm, and moved forward.

The contrast goes far beyond tactics.

**The Mirror of Reality**

Ancient Greeks put “Know thyself” on the Temple of Delphi for a reason. Real self-knowledge means clearly seeing your actual capabilities, limitations, and baseline, whether as an individual or as an organization.

Most of us, and most organizations, struggle with this. When results are bad, the pressure to do something visible becomes overwhelming. We prefer dramatic action over quiet, honest assessment.

This is what happened with Tunisia. Sacking the coach was less about strategy and more like feeding a gladiator to the lions: a public sacrifice to appease the crowd and deflect from deeper problems. Even if the move had miraculously worked and Tunisia advanced, it would still have been the wrong approach. That was likely their realistic ceiling. The sacking did not fix the system. It just changed the name on the door.

**Cultural Distortions**

Living between cultures has made this pattern clear to me. Some environments turn pressure into frantic, symbolic moves. Others, like the Swedish setup, maintain a steadier baseline. They separate a bad result from an existential threat and assess reality first.

**The Product Lesson**

We see the same pattern in tech. A launch fails, panic sets in, and a Head of Product or CEO is sacrificed to calm investors and show action.

These performative resets rarely change the outcome. They usually make things worse by destroying institutional knowledge and adding chaos.

Elite product craft demands a steady baseline. When pressure hits, you can look honestly into the mirror of your real capabilities or keep feeding the gladiators to the crowd.

True substance chooses the mirror.
