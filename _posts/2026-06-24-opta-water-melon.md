During the World Cup group stages, Turkish defender Abdülkerim Bardakci completed all 98 passes he attempted against Paraguay, the highest number with perfect accuracy recorded in a single match since 1966.

Turkey lost. Then the team was eliminated after two games, with a brutal collective stat: [62 shots attempted, 0 goals scored](https://sports.yahoo.com/articles/turkey-face-paraguay-both-bidding-224348899.html).

A flawless individual record. A collective failure. This is the Watermelon Effect.

**Green on the Outside**

In corporate management, a *watermelon project* is one where every dashboard metric glows green. Engineers hit sprint velocity, designers deliver on time, individual contributors execute with precision.

Management sees the wall of green and celebrates.

But slice the watermelon open, and it's red inside. The product launches, but it doesn't convert users. The system is stable, but it solves zero real customer problems.

This happens because organizations measure what's easy to track, not what drives the actual outcome. Pass completion is a clean, satisfying number. Shot volume looks like progress. But in football, as in business, only one number changes reality: the score.

**The Product Lesson**

Tech teams do this constantly. They ship fifty features in a quarter while retention quietly collapses. They count shots without scoring goals, passing the ball safely in their own half, mistaking activity for impact.

Real product craft means looking past superficial precision. Don't get comforted by perfect internal stats while the outcome is failing your users.

Stop measuring the passing streaks. Start looking at the score.
